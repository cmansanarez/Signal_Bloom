# Toward Emergence: Early Reflections on My Capstone Direction

Over the past few weeks, I’ve been trying to identify what I actually want my capstone project to become. Not just technically, but artistically and philosophically. I knew from the beginning that I wanted the work to involve live visuals, performance, and generative systems because those are the spaces where I feel most creatively alive. What I didn’t fully realize yet was that the project itself would eventually become about the tension between control and unpredictability.

Initially, I approached the ideation process from the perspective of VJing and live coding. I’ve been increasingly drawn to Hydra because of its immediacy and instability. There is something uniquely exciting about manipulating visuals live through code while responding to sound, atmosphere, and audience energy in real time. Unlike pre-rendered visuals, live coding feels vulnerable. The process is exposed. Mistakes become part of the performance. Systems evolve unpredictably. That tension feels important to me.

As my thinking around the project evolved, I started realizing that the capstone should not simply be “a live visual performance.” Instead, it should investigate a larger conceptual question:

# Thesis Statement
> the evolving relationship between performer, machine, and emergence. 

That statement has become the central thesis for the project and the lens through which I’m now evaluating every technical and artistic decision moving forward.

The more I reflected on my current workflow, the more I realized I wanted to challenge it. Right now, much of my visual process involves generating images or loops in Midjourney ahead of time and then incorporating them into performances. While that process can produce compelling imagery, it still relies heavily on pre-authored assets. I became interested in what would happen if generation itself became performative.

That realization fundamentally shifted the direction of the project.

I started imagining a modular AI-assisted live visual performance environment where Hydra live coding, generative systems, and AI image or video generation could coexist inside one evolving ecosystem. In this system, I could live code audio-reactive textures in Hydra while simultaneously invoking AI-generated imagery in real time through prompts entered during the performance itself.

For example, I might type:

“Generate a three-dimensional torus with iridescent chrome spikes.”

The system would then generate that visual object dynamically and route it directly into the live visual environment, where it could be manipulated, glitched, distorted, layered, or recursively fed back into itself through Hydra. Suddenly, the performance would no longer just be about controlling visuals. It would become an improvisational collaboration between performer, machine systems, sound, audience energy, and emergence.

That idea immediately felt much more aligned with my artistic practice.

A major realization for me was understanding that I am not simply interested in “making visuals.” I am interested in designing conditions for emergence. That distinction feels incredibly important. The project is becoming less about finalized outputs and more about systems behavior, improvisation, instability, and transformation.

This also connects deeply to my broader artistic interests around queerness, glitch aesthetics, cybernetic systems, futurism, resistance, and identity. I’ve long been drawn to glitches because they feel political to me. A glitch interrupts expected behavior. It exposes hidden systems. It destabilizes dominant structures. In many ways, queerness itself can operate similarly: as a disruption of imposed systems and fixed categories. I want the work to inhabit that unstable space between collapse and transformation.

Another important development has been realizing that the system itself may actually become the artwork. Rather than treating AI as a novelty or gimmick, I’m becoming more interested in framing it as a collaborative generative entity within the performance. The artwork exists not in any single generated image, but in the evolving dialogue between human improvisation and machine behavior.

Technically, I’m now beginning to think more seriously about how this system could be constructed. I’ve become particularly interested in local AI generation workflows and systems like ComfyUI, especially because they allow more modularity and experimentation than closed commercial platforms. I plan to speak with my Program Director soon to discuss the feasibility of integrating local AI image or video generation into a live Hydra-based environment. Right now, I think one of the most important challenges will be balancing conceptual ambition with technical scope.

At this stage, the project still feels fluid and emergent, which is probably appropriate given the themes I’m exploring. But for the first time, I feel like I’m not just brainstorming disconnected ideas. I feel like I’m beginning to identify the core artistic language that ties together my interests in live coding, generative systems, AI, performance, glitch aesthetics, queer futurism, and immersive media.

More than anything, this process is helping me realize that the capstone is not just a final project. It is becoming a reflection of how I think about technology, authorship, identity, emergence, and creative practice itself.
